The Results

Brief Overview

This was an experiment to see whether the general population leans more towards a time-moving or an ego-moving way of thinking. If you would like to learn more about these concepts, check out our time metaphors page where we talk about them at length.

The experiment was conducted by assigning each participant to one of three paths. The first path required the participant to answer our main question blindly. The second and third paths showed each participant a video, intending to prime them to think in either time-moving or ego-moving terms before asking them our question. We then logged this data to determine two things:

whether there seemed to be a preference for one of way thinking over another, and whether or not priming the participants pushed them more strongly towards a certain way of thinking.

Our results are thus divided into three parts, showing both our blind path results, and the results of our primed paths.

The Data

Even before breaking off into the different paths, the results showed an overwhelming preference for thinking in terms of time-moving. When asked if a meeting, initially scheduled for Wednesday, was pushed back two days, the majority of people polled said that that would mean that the meeting was now scheduled for Friday.

Moving further into our priimed paths, out of all the participants that were primed with our ego-moving video, not a single one of them seemed to be affected by it. Every participant from that group answered Friday, meaning that the way they think is very strongly time moving.

Out of our last group, the one that was primed to think in time-moving terms, the majority answered in time-moving terms, as expected. A small percentage, 28.57%, answered in ego-moving terms, meaning that they felt the meeting should now be held on Monday.
